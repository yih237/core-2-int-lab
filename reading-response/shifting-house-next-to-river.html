Reading Response 3
Response to: My website is a shifting house next to a river of knowledge. What could yours be?
by Laurel Schwulst

Laurel Schwurst's writing was an easy and pleasant read compared to the other readings but was still informational. Schwulst uses different analogies and metaphors, showing the different ways that different websites can grow. It also shows the diverse range of how a website can serve as both an art form and a technological tool. She also reminded the audience that we should not be afraid of the web and instead looked at it as somewhere where artists create. I like the analogy of the garden, as sometimes websites burst out all of a sudden, like the autumn season, but sometimes the gardener(designer) has to work on it for a long time to get a breakthrough, like in the winter. Schwulst promoted us not to be afraid of the web and instead do anything we want, even making sites like a rock thrown into the ocean or a rain puddle that dries after a bit. The web has unlimited possibilities; she states, "the web is what you made of it." She also wanted us not to view the internet as it is a common prevailing metaphor â€” a cloud.


This article has drastically altered my perception of the internet. I always had a perception while designing and coding that I was just "not good enough" before reading this article. There was a category of "good" websites, and mine wasn't one of them. I always wanted more from the websites I created. However, as Schwulst stated, a website can be unexpected, boring, subtle, or bold... And I like the idea that websites are always unfinished. Websites are like art, they are always waiting for users to complete them -- there's never a complete website, nor is there ever a complete piece of art.


After reading this article, I realized the importance of the relationship between creator and audience and how accessibility, user experience, and maintenance are crucial to a website.
